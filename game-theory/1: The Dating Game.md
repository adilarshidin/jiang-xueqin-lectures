## Game theory

The best way to understand human behaviour, nations, societies.

Game has 3 components: players, rules, incentives.

Knowing 3 components one can understand how the game works and will end.

## Example

5 male and female players ranked according to genes (attractiveness), wealth, status.

|Male|Female|
|----|------|
|5|5|
|4|4|
|3|3|
|2|2|
|1|1|

The best strategy for all the players would be to take their number. Nash equilibrium - you always reach a state where all the players maximize their outcome. However in real life nobody follows the rule.

All 5 females try to procreate with number 5 and maybe 4 but others are out of scope.

And 5 male will go for all the females.

People are rational but you need to understand the game. Here the incentive is not the sex itself or family but status/wealth for women (women are "independent" and childbirth is hard so the man needs to be attractive and/or wealthy). The game changed.

1. Low population society like a village. No competition, poor. Here people do not date, they marry and have children.
2. Growing population society. Wealth, high competition. No dating, need to have as much people as possible to compete with other societies, arranged marriages for that.
3. Overpopulation. Too much wealth (inequality), equilibrium. Dating because to change status you marry up, no other way to do it, decreasing fertility.

## P.S.

This lecture is right that game theory matters. Players, rules, and incentives explain behavior better than morals or ideology. But what’s missing is that once a game runs long enough, the incentives stop being abstract. They rewire biology, psychology, and daily stress until behavior becomes automatic.

In modern societies the main incentive is money and status, not because people are greedy, but because money became the universal access token to safety. Housing, healthcare, education, dignity, time, and future options all go through markets. Communal buffers are gone. So the real game is not “get rich” but “don’t fall.” When inequality is high, falling isn’t a step down, it’s a cliff. Biologically, that reads as threat.
Constant threat means chronic stress. Cortisol stays high, dopamine systems shift toward short-term validation, novelty, and comparison. Long-term bonding weakens, fertility hormones drop. People delay commitment and children, not because they hate family, but because under stress the human system postpones reproduction. Psychology then rationalizes it as preference, but the driver is regulation.
Status today means position in a hierarchy that decides how safe your life is. Algorithms force endless upward social comparison. Richer, prettier, more successful lives are always visible. Status becomes zero-sum and unstable. “Good enough” stops feeling safe. So in the dating game people don’t optimize for love, they optimize against risk. This is why the Nash equilibrium model breaks in real life. Real societies are not clean markets, they are manipulated status arenas.

Poor countries don’t hit this wall yet because the game is different. Survival is collective. Extended family buffers risk. Children add resilience instead of vulnerability. Comparison is local, not global. Reproduction still makes biological sense there, even under hardship.
Now here’s the part history keeps screaming and everyone ignores. Systems like this always overproduce elites. Too many credentialed aspirants, too many status seekers, too few real positions. The middle buffer gets squeezed. Downward mobility fear explodes. Elite competition intensifies while the productive base erodes. Rome did this when smallholders disappeared. Other empires did it when wealth concentrated and titles outpaced real capacity. The pattern is old.

Elite overproduction plus inequality turns society into a pressure cooker. Everyone competes upward. Nobody wants to reproduce downward. Fertility collapses. Community dissolves. Stress becomes permanent. Consumerism and platforms profit because they monetize anxiety and comparison. Asset holders benefit because precarity keeps people compliant. Governments try bonuses, but bonuses don’t change the rules of the game.

This isn’t men vs. women or morals failing. It’s structure. When safety is tied to hierarchy and status, and the system overproduces elites while hollowing out the middle, the outcome is always the same. Humans adapt perfectly. The civilization doesn’t.
Game theory explains the mechanics exactly as the lecture says: players, rules, incentives. But unless you change the incentives and stop elite overproduction, the game always ends the same way. The middle collapses, fertility drops, the dependency ratio explodes, and internal competition intensifies. The state tries to compensate with debt, coercion, or narrative control, while elites turn on each other as upward mobility stalls. Eventually pressure has to be released. Historically that release comes through external conflict or internal violence. War resets hierarchies, destroys excess capital, absorbs surplus elites, and reasserts control over populations. If war is not possible, the system drifts into stagnation and decay until fragmentation or collapse follows. Not because people are stupid, but because the structure trains everyone to optimize short-term survival while liquidating the future.
